# Mipolai — Studio Strategy & Bathhouse Launch Plan

**What this is:** a strategy conversation (with Claude, 22 May 2026) about turning
Mipolai from "a person with apps" into a studio that builds a world — covering
positioning, which formats to expand into and in what order, monetization, and a
concrete launch plan for the second app (**Bathhouse** — the app this repo,
codename *Playtime*, is being built for).

It is **not marketing copy**. It's the strategic backdrop: why Bathhouse matters
beyond being a product, and how to launch it as a *studio* rather than a one-off.

---

## Key decisions / takeaways

- **It's a studio building a world, not a company shipping formats.** Protect a
  core (the Mipos, the hand-drawn ad-free aesthetic) and let formats radiate from
  it (Ghibli model, not CoComelon). Every format must feel like it came from the
  same hands, or the whole thing dilutes.
- **Bathhouse is a "Mipo factory" and a content engine.** Puppet Maker lets people
  *build* Mipos; Bathhouse *reveals* them (wash a ball of mud → discover a named
  character). Every discovery is a 20-second clip — content as a byproduct of play.
- **Format expansion order:** short video first (closest to what's already made,
  feeds every surface), picture book second (permanence + credibility, monetizes
  the characters, de-risks App-Store dependence), full YouTube channel a distant
  third (highest effort, easiest to ruin — only with an algorithm-resisting format).
- **Solo is a hard constraint** that should drive every decision. No weekly-cadence
  commitments (channels, newsletters). Build things that benefit from slowness.
- **Monetization, no-ads/no-IAP line protected.** "No IAP" ≠ "no paid extras."
  Lean: **Bathhouse free with a one-time unlock (~€2.99)** after 2–3 Mipos
  discovered. Puppet Maker stays free as the gateway. Annual paid packs and a book
  later; patronage and licensing much later (let them pull, don't push).
- **Bathhouse ships in ~2–3 months → this is a launch, not a year-plan.** Treat it
  as a **studio launch disguised as an app launch.** The press story is "Amsterdam
  studio Mipolai releases its second ad-free hand-drawn app," which retroactively
  makes Puppet Maker "the first app."
- **The single highest-leverage move is an Apple editorial feature** (needs ~6–8
  weeks lead time → pitch window opens immediately).
- **mipolai.com needs a redirect, not a rebuild** (~1–2 weeks): hold two apps as
  equals, add a studio paragraph + a press page, add a Bathhouse teaser page.
  Do **not** sand off the voice/poem/weirdness.
- **3 downloads/day isn't a demand problem, it's an awareness problem.** The apps
  have had zero oxygen (no feature, no press, no launch moment). The Bathhouse
  launch is the test of what they do under real conditions.

## The conversation

### Q1 — "How do I approach building a children's-media company? What do you already know about me?"

**Context Claude had:** Mipolai, a one-person Amsterdam studio making children's
media with a distinctive hand-drawn, weird, no-ads/no-IAP aesthetic. *Mipo Puppet
Maker* out on iOS (free, no ads, no microtransactions), built on a custom physics
engine. A second project codenamed *Playtime*, under wraps. A son with a small
YouTube channel. Long-running thinking about an Aphex-Twin-style mythology —
fragments scattered across surfaces, depth over breadth, found rather than delivered.

**The reframe:** This isn't "a company that makes children's media" — it's a studio
building a world. Companies optimize for distribution across formats; studios
protect a core and let formats radiate. Pixar nearly died as a hardware company;
what saved it was that characters and stories were the product and format was
downstream. Ghibli is the clean example — one world, one set of hands, expressed
across films, books, a museum, a park, aesthetic never compromised. Mipolai is
closer to that than to CoComelon.

Why it matters: fanning out into YouTube + books + apps + shorts tempts you to
treat each as a separate product line — that way lies dilution. The Mipos working
across formats only works if every format feels like the same hands. The moment a
short looks like generic kids' YouTube, the whole edifice weakens — even the apps.

**Sequencing > scope.** Each new format is a craft commitment (a real picture book
is ~a year; a non-terrible YouTube channel is multi-year), and they pull in
opposite directions (YouTube rewards volume/consistency; picture books reward
slowness/precision). Pick **one** expansion in the next 12 months, do it properly,
then add another. The two apps + website + son's channel are *already* a small
media company — you don't need to "start," you need to name what exists and decide
where the next investment goes.

**Expansion order (most leverage, least aesthetic risk):**
1. **Storytelling / short video** — 30–90s Mipo animations, no narration, weird and
   watchable. Closest to what's already made, easiest to keep on-brand, feeds
   Instagram/Vimeo/site/App-Store at once, doesn't context-switch from app dev.
2. **A picture book** — the Mipos are already picture-book characters who happen to
   live in apps. The picture-book world is hunting for this. One book with the
   right indie publisher (Flying Eye, Enchanted Lion, Lemniscaat NL) creates
   permanence apps can't, and credentials the studio to journalists/schools/parents.
3. **A full YouTube channel** — highest effort, easiest to ruin (the algorithm
   rewards frantic over-stimulation, the opposite of the aesthetic). Only enter
   with a format that *resists* the algorithm (slow stop-motion, quiet narration,
   ambient music — *In the Night Garden*, not CoComelon). Shorts on IG/TikTok may
   do more than a channel would.

Claude asked three questions before going further: what's the second app, solo or
with collaborators, and what's the business-model intent.

### Q2 — "Bathhouse: a ball of mud enters, you wash it off, it bathes, you discover a new Mipo. Solo. I want some way to make money — no ads, maybe in-app extras or non-free apps."

**On the concept:**
- **It's a Mipo factory.** Puppet Maker = player-driven Mipo creation; Bathhouse =
  author-driven Mipo *reveal*. Together they start to feel like one universe. It's
  also the first app where you, as author, introduce *named* characters with
  personalities — exactly what's needed before books/video/narrative.
- **It's a content engine.** Every discovered Mipo is inherently watchable content
  that needs no narration or translation and works on every surface. The app
  *produces* content as a byproduct — no separate "make content" effort.

**On solo + money:** Solo is a hard constraint that should drive everything.
- A truly solo picture book is ~18 months, not 12, unless a publisher brings an
  editor and handles production.
- A YouTube channel as a primary discipline is off the table. Shorts as a byproduct
  of the apps — fine. A channel you feed weekly — no.
- Anything needing sustained weekly cadence is dangerous (one missed week and it
  decays). Build things that benefit from slowness.

**Monetization shapes (no-ads/no-IAP line protected; "no IAP" ≠ "no paid extras"),
roughly by fit:**
1. **Paid apps, free to try.** Cleanest. Bathhouse paid up front (€3–5) or free with
   a one-time unlock (€2–3) after a couple of Mipos. A paywall, not a slot machine —
   Apple editorial favors it, parents trust it. Puppet Maker stays the free gateway.
   Realistic: hundreds to low-thousands €/month at modest scale, more if featured.
2. **Mipo-of-the-month / annual pack.** One paid expansion per year per app (new
   bath types, puppet parts, a seasonal Mipo). Buy once, keep forever — how indie
   devs survive (Townscaper, A Short Hike).
3. **A book.** Push hardest on this once Bathhouse ships: it monetizes the
   *characters* (protecting the apps from carrying revenue alone), has margins indie
   devs envy (advance + royalties via a publisher), is a 10-year marketing surface
   in a child's bedroom, and de-risks App-Store dependence. Partner with an indie
   publisher (Flying Eye, Enchanted Lion, Lemniscaat, Querido), don't self-publish.
4. **Patronage — later, not now.** Once enough Mipos exist that people care, a small
   "Friends of Mipolai" tier (€3–5/mo, named in apps, early peeks, annual zine).
   Premature patronage feels like begging; well-timed feels like a club.
5. **Licensing — much later.** Stickers, pins, plushies. Let it pull (someone offers
   to manufacture), don't push.

**Specifically do NOT:** subscriptions to the apps; "premium" Mipos flashier than
free ones; any two-tier experience for kids inside the app.

**Rough 12-month sequence:** Months 1–6 ship Bathhouse (paid or free-with-unlock),
accumulating discovery footage as a byproduct (don't post yet). At launch, pitch
Apple seriously (App Store Promotion Request, 6–8 weeks lead). Months 6–9, start the
video drip from accumulated footage (IG/Vimeo, no captions/hashtag-strategy).
Months 9–12, start the *book conversation* (Bologna Children's Book Fair, spring) —
find the publisher, don't deliver a manuscript. Year 2: book in production, third
app concept, maybe open Friends of Mipolai, maybe a first physical object.
*Principle: every move should monetize what exists or feed the next thing; no move
should require becoming a different kind of company.*

### Q3 — "Realistically, ~2–3 months to ship."

That changes it from a year-plan to a **launch plan** — everything queues behind it.
This is the first chance to launch as **Mipolai-the-studio**, not a one-app dev.
The story the second time has a body of work, an aesthetic, a second piece of
evidence it isn't a fluke — which is what journalists and Apple editors respond to.

**The frame: a studio launch disguised as an app launch.** Press story = "Amsterdam
studio Mipolai releases its second app in a hand-drawn, ad-free universe — a
bathhouse where you wash mud to discover hidden characters." It retroactively makes
Puppet Maker "the first app," signals more is coming, and gives writers something
bigger than one product.

Three things should exist before launch: a **studio page** on mipolai.com (lists
both apps, two sentences on who you are, reads like an ongoing thing); a **one-page
studio PDF** for pitches (name, location, what you make, two apps one line each,
your name, contact); and a decision on whether **the world has a name** (like Moomin
Valley) — not necessarily settled, but thought about.

**Weeks 1–4 — build launch assets while finishing the app:**
- **A 30–45s trailer.** Mood piece, not a feature list: muddy ball enters → washed →
  character emerges → walks off. Music, no narration. The single most important
  asset (App Store page, pitch embeds, top of any article). *If you make one good
  thing this month, make this.*
- **5–10 short clips (10–20s each)** of different Mipos emerging — the post-launch
  drip + Apple-feature supporting media. Capture as you play.
- **A press kit folder** (Dropbox/Drive link): trailer, 10 stills, full-res icon,
  logo, the studio PDF, two paragraphs of liftable "about" copy. Make writing about
  you the easiest possible job.
- **App Store screenshots + copy.** ~Half of installs come from the page itself.
  Screenshots should feel like the app, not ads-with-text. Copy in your voice,
  short, no "Features:" bullet list.

**Weeks 4–6 — pitches go out** (timing matters most; Apple wants 6–8 weeks,
journalists 2–4):
- **Apple App Store Promotion Request** (via App Store Connect). Pitch = the studio
  story with the app as the hook; offer exclusivity if possible; include the
  trailer. "Solo Dutch dev, two ad-free hand-drawn apps on a custom physics engine,
  second launching" is the whole pitch and it's strong.
- **3–5 personalized journalist pitches** (not a blast): design press (Eye on
  Design, It's Nice That, The Creative Independent), Maria Popova / The Marginalian
  if there's a hook, one Dutch design writer, one picture-book person (Picturebook
  Makers blog). One short paragraph + trailer link + press-kit link, referencing
  something specific they wrote.
- **One Substack pitch** (Anya Kamenetz, Anne Helen Petersen, anti-slop parent
  world): framed as "how I make kids' media that isn't garbage, here's the evidence."
- **Don't** pitch generic tech press (TechCrunch/Verge) — wrong audience and frame.

**Weeks 6–8 — launch week (matters more than launch day):** don't launch Friday
(Tue/Wed convention); give existing Puppet Maker users something (push or an update
mentioning Bathhouse) — they're the likeliest first installers and reviewers;
reviews in the first 72h are disproportionately important; post the trailer **once,
everywhere** on launch day (the one moment direct broadcast beats "fragments").

**Pricing — decide within ~2 weeks** (affects copy + pitch framing):
1. Paid up front, €3.99/4.99 — cleanest, Apple loves it, highest-quality audience,
   immediate revenue; fewer installs = less algorithmic momentum.
2. **Free with a one-time unlock at €2.99 after 2–3 Mipos** — wide try, narrow buy,
   nothing recurring; fits the "extras" idea; the unlock must feel generous, not a
   wall. **Claude's recommendation** — matches Puppet Maker's free expectation,
   introduces that Mipolai apps cost something without breaking no-ads, gives a
   clean revenue number for the next pitch.
3. Free with a paid expansion pack later (e.g. "Hot Springs", €2.99) — lowest launch
   revenue, highest installs, pack = second news moment; only if confident you'll
   ship it.

**Pushback:** don't try to do everything. A polished app on time with a good trailer
and a tight Apple pitch beats a slightly better app with no launch infrastructure.
The aesthetic only compounds if people see it; the launch is the showing.

### Q4 — "Check mipolai.com and decide."

**What it is now:** a Puppet Maker landing page with strong personality. The voice is
right (AD FREE / CRAP FREE / MICRO TRANSACTION FREE; the Mipo poem; weird
testimonials; "father of 2 making apps for my kids and yours" — the best one-line
studio pitch possible). Aesthetic intact. But structurally it's set up as if Puppet
Maker is the only thing; Bathhouse can't just be another button.

**Change before launch (a redirect, not a rebuild — ~1–2 weeks, ideally weeks 3–5):**
- **Homepage holds two apps as equals** — not a generic "Our Apps" grid (kills the
  voice), but e.g. "Puppet Maker — build a Mipo from weird pieces. Bathhouse — wash a
  ball of mud and meet whoever's inside." Two lines, two buttons, poem stays.
- **A real studio paragraph**, prominent — who/where you are, what you make, what you
  don't do. In your voice, but it must exist.
- **A press surface** (mipolai.com/press or /studio): studio paragraph, two apps,
  logo, trailer, 5–10 screenshots, contact, the one-page PDF. So a writer Googling
  you doesn't land on a Puppet Maker page and have to assemble the studio themselves.
- **A Bathhouse teaser page** (same style) live *before* launch — where the trailer
  link points in pitches and pre-launch coverage.
- **Surface the existing interview** ("Nikki on kids, iPads, and why Mipos smell
  weird") — currently buried at the bottom; it's exactly what journalists love.

**Do NOT change:** the voice, the poem, the deliberately-weird testimonials, the
"1980" parenthetical, the lowercase "blub text" link, the stray sjoep4 mp4 fragment,
the slightly-broken English. If a designer says it looks too handmade, ignore them —
the handmade quality is doing work polish would undo, and design press + Apple
editorial respond to it *because* it doesn't look like everyone's Notion-page landing.

**Decision + order:** redirect, not rebuild. (1) Build the Bathhouse teaser page;
(2) write the studio paragraph and place it on the homepage; (3) restructure the
homepage to hold two apps without losing the voice; (4) build the press page last
(by then trailer + screenshots exist). It's a static site — don't move to a CMS,
don't redesign; add and reframe. Offer: have Claude read the new homepage/studio
copy with the *voice* question specifically in mind (the risk in a rewrite is the
voice going 10% more "professional" and losing what works).

### Q5 — "Marketing: Puppet Maker gets ~2–3 downloads/day (free). That's not enough to build anything."

**The honest part:** correct — ~900/year isn't a business. Even at 10× for a great
Bathhouse launch, the math doesn't make it primary income. A paid app at €3.99
doing 30/day ≈ €2,500/month after Apple's cut — real money, not an Amsterdam salary.
So "can I live off this at current numbers?" — no, and you knew that.

**The pushback — wrong benchmark:** Puppet Maker does 2–3/day with *zero* marketing —
no ads, no influencer push, no Apple feature, no real press, no live launch moment,
a landing page that doesn't fully sell it, and no review velocity for the algorithm
to feed on. Under those conditions, anything above zero is evidence the product is
good. The real comparison is "3/day with no marketing" vs. what the apps do after
one of: an Apple feature (typically 10–50× during, 3–5× residual after); a real
design/parent-press hit (2-week spike, permanent awareness change); the Bathhouse
launch lifting the back catalog; or the two apps cross-promoting (a banner in Puppet
Maker should ~double Bathhouse installs). None have happened — **you don't yet know
what the apps do under normal conditions, only with no oxygen.**

**The bigger reframe:** the value of Mipolai isn't measured in downloads (or you
wouldn't have asked about books and video). The apps are the visible part; what's
being built is a body of work and a recognizable aesthetic, which compound on a
different timescale. (Beatrix Potter had no viral year one; Tove Jansson ran Moomin
strips for years pre-phenomenon; Ghibli's first film lost money.) What actually
signals it's working: do finders *love* it (reviews say yes); does each release get
better (Bathhouse > Puppet Maker, yes); is the aesthetic recognizable (yes); are
there adjacent surfaces for the same world (yes). If those are yes, the problem is
"not enough people know," not "the apps don't work" — different problems.

**What moves the number, in order:** (1) an Apple feature — the biggest lever and the
most on-aesthetic; pitch it around the Bathhouse launch; (2) press in the *right*
outlets (design + picture-book/parenting niche, not tech press); (3) cross-promotion
between your own apps (free, trivial, powerful); (4) a second piece of evidence the
studio is real — what Bathhouse itself does (one→two apps is a bigger psychological
jump for a journalist/editor than two→three); (5) a picture book, eventually (the
long, permanent lever; takes you off App-Store dependence).

**What doesn't move it at this scale:** paid ads, social volume, hashtag strategy,
growth hacking, "going viral." Those optimize *existing* demand; the problem here is
awareness, not demand.

**The answer:** 3/day isn't enough to build on, and it isn't enough to *judge* by —
wrong data, too little of it. The apps are in a vacuum. Put oxygen in the room (the
Bathhouse launch, done well) and see what happens. If three months post-launch
they're still at 3/day, the marketing problem is real and worth rethinking — and
even then the answer is almost certainly *not* "stop making this," but "apps are the
wrong primary surface for this audience; bring the book/physical objects sooner."
**The aesthetic is the asset; the format is negotiable.**
