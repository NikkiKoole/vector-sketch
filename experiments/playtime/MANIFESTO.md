# The Sago Rule

A manifesto for the small-apps-for-young-children pivot.

---

**One core interaction per app.**
Sago Mini's apps each do *one thing*: the post office, the road trip, the pet vet. Not three things. One. Pick the verb (sort, build, feed, dress, deliver) before anything else, and protect it.

**Four to six weeks, then ship.**
If it's not shipping in that window, the scope is wrong — not the deadline. Cut features, not corners.

**No "and also".**
The moment a feature starts with "and also it could…", that feature belongs to the next app. Write it down and walk away.

**If app #1 wants to grow, that's app #2.**
A second mechanic is not a bonus. It's an admission that you don't trust the first mechanic. Trust the first mechanic.

**Shipping beats scope.**
Ten small shipped things teach you more — about the audience, the App Store, what kids actually do — than one unshipped masterpiece. Mipo proved you can ship. Knut proved long arcs don't fit. Optimise for the shape of brain you have, not the brain you wish you had.

**The toolchain is not the product.**
Playtime, the IAP layer, the Mipo character system — these are the foundation, not the deliverable. Resist the urge to keep polishing the workshop instead of making the chair.

**Each app is its own win.**
Don't make app #1 a stepping stone to app #5. Each one stands alone, ships alone, succeeds or fails alone. That's how the portfolio compounds.

**World juice is not a second verb.**
A rubber duck bobbing in the bath is not a mechanic. A floaty boat that the sponge-wake pushes around is not a mechanic. These are physics making the world feel inhabited. The rule protects the *verb* — the intentional action with a goal and a consequence. Ambient things that respond to the kid's presence without having their own goal or lifecycle are texture, not scope. The line: if it has a win state, a dirty/clean state, or needs a tutorial — it's a mechanic. If it just exists and reacts — it's world juice. Add it freely.
