  1. THE MIPO WORLD
================================================================================

The Mipo World feels like someone doodled a physics textbook in a 1970s
children's encyclopedia, then blew life into the margins. Everything is
hand-drawn and slightly rubbery: clouds bounce, shadows wiggle, and "gravity"
is more of a polite suggestion than a rule.

Mipos — semi-sentient, potato-head collage-creatures — are the stars of this
squishy pocket-universe. They come apart, snap together, stretch, repaint, or
sprout new gadgets at the player's whim, and each new combination might unlock
a fresh ability or a slapstick disaster.

Seven regions:
  - MipoTown — toaster-houses and dancing doors, the social hub
  - The Playground — build-your-own Rube-Goldberg of trampolines and cannons
  - Forest of Limbs — a wardrobe made of living body parts
  - Wobbly Hills — walking becomes bowling
  - Sky-Sketch Isles — toast-gliders and spaghetti-copters
  - Burrow Below — a tinkerer's maze of scrap
  - Busybody Bay — earnest nonsense parody of grown-up jobs

Mipos themselves are open-hearted slapstick inventors. Their emotions broadcast
physically — eyes balloon when startled, limbs pop off when annoyed, whole
bodies bounce with joy. Their myths are equally silly: a giggle-powered sun, a
sleepy moon that sneezed the world into being, and a legendary Big Box toy
store where perfect Mipos are displayed forever.


================================================================================
  2. DESIGN PHILOSOPHY
================================================================================

"Serious nonsense" — the language should feel playful and meaningful at the
same time. Like toddler babble that makes sense if you listen carefully.

Principles:
  - Simple CV (consonant-vowel) syllables, like a toddler's first words
  - Semantic compositionality: complex meanings built from small roots
  - Designed for speech synthesis, mouth animation, and emotional expression
  - Modularity mirrors the Mipo creatures themselves (snap together, pull apart)
  - Singable, memorable, and fun to speak aloud


================================================================================
  3. PHONOLOGY
================================================================================

Structure: strict CV (consonant + vowel). No consonant clusters, no closed
syllables. Every syllable is open.

Consonants (13): B  D  F  H  J  K  L  M  N  P  S  T  W
Vowels (3):      A  I  O

This gives 39 possible syllables:

  BA  BI  BO        DA  DI  DO        FA  FI  FO
  HA  HI  HO        JA  JI  JO        KA  KI  KO
  LA  LI  LO        MA  MI  MO        NA  NI  NO
  PA  PI  PO        SA  SI  SO        TA  TI  TO
  WA  WI  WO

Each syllable is recorded as a discrete audio sample (in multiple voices).
Pitch, volume, and timing are modulated at runtime for prosody.

  4. THE SEMANTIC GRID — 13 x 3 Core Roots
================================================================================

Every syllable has a meaning. The consonant determines the semantic field,
the vowel shifts meaning within it.

+--------------------------+---------------------------+---------------------------+---------------------------+
| FIELD                  C |   -A                      |   -I                      |   -O                      |
+--------------------------+---------------------------+---------------------------+---------------------------+
| self / feeling         M | MA  nurture               | MI  I / self              | MO  love / feel           |
| living beings          B | BA  life / child           | BI  peer / kin / they     | BO  animal / creature     |
| possession / transfer  P | PA  keep / hold            | PI  take / receive        | PO  give                  |
| nature / element       F | FA  fire / heat            | FI  earth / soil          | FO  sky / air             |
| thing / place          K | KA  object / stuff         | KI  tool / device         | KO  place / container     |
| beauty / design        L | LA  light / good           | LI  fine / detail         | LO  shape / form          |
| time / goal            T | TA  past                   | TI  now                   | TO  future                |
| voice / story          J | JA  speak                  | JI  name / identity       | JO  you / listener        |
| negation / rest        N | NA  none / absence         | NI  rest / sleep          | NO  end / negate          |
| body / substance       D | DA  body / flesh           | DI  eat / drink           | DO  stone / rock          |
| mind / pattern         S | SA  idea                   | SI  sign / tag            | SO  pattern / structure   |
| action / control       H | HA  start                  | HI  go / act              | HO  stop / halt           |
| water / qualities      W | WA  water / flow           | WI  cold / ice            | WO  round / solid         |
+--------------------------+---------------------------+---------------------------+---------------------------+

Vowel tendencies across the grid:
  -A  tends toward the raw, energetic, or source
  -I  tends toward the precise, individual, or internal
  -O  tends toward the abstract, spatial, or outward


================================================================================
  5. GRAMMAR
================================================================================

PRONOUNS
  mi    I / me          (from MI "self")
  jo    you             (from JO "listener")
  bi    they / them     (from BI "peer/kin")

ASPECT PARTICLES (placed immediately after the subject)
  i     STATE / BE      — something simply is (identity, description, location)
  a     BECOME / MOVE   — entering a new state, heading toward a goal
  o     DO / ACT        — deliberate, ongoing action

  Mnemonics:
    i = high, steady vowel => stable state
    a = mouth opens, you "step forward" => change
    o = round, forceful sound => energy, action

NEGATION
  na    NOT — placed before the aspect particle
        mi na i lala  =>  "I am not good"

WORD ORDER
  Loose S-V-O, but the aspect particle anchors the clause.
  You can chain aspect particles for layered meaning:
    mi i moki o di widi  =>  "I (state: wanting) (do: eat) ice-cream"

SENTENCE TEMPLATE
  (Subject) (na?) (Aspect) (Predicate roots) (location/time) (paragrammar)


================================================================================
  6. COMPOUND WORD-ORDER RULE
================================================================================

Concrete nouns are HEAD-FIRST — the category root leads:
  KO-BIPA  = house       (place + kin + keep)
  KI-FIHI  = car         (tool + earth + go)
  BO-FO    = bird        (creature + sky)
  DA-KA    = arm         (body + object)

Function words & spatial terms are MODIFIER-FIRST — the quality leads:
  FO-KO    = up          (sky + place)
  FI-KO    = down        (earth + place)
  MO-LA    = joy         (feel + light)
  LA-LA    = good        (light + light)

This means: if you're naming a THING, start with what kind of thing it is.
If you're describing a QUALITY or DIRECTION, start with the quality.


================================================================================
  7. REDUPLICATION
================================================================================

Doubling a root intensifies or iterates:
  MOMO     love (intense feeling)
  HIHI     run (go+go)
  NINI     very sleepy (rest+rest)
  SOSO     spin (pattern+pattern)
  MISO MISO = sob hard / keep sobbing

Reduplication is also the basis for COLORS (see lexicon section 11).


================================================================================
  8. PARAGRAMMAR CONSONANTS
================================================================================

Single consonants whispered after a sentence add emotional color:

  M   comfort, warmth
  P   readiness, tension
  T   urgency
  H   sigh, awe
  F   frustration
  B   affectionate "aww"
  N   negation
  D   firm closure

Example: "mi i momo... m."  (I am in love... [warm sigh])

Other notable reduplications as standalone expressions:
  POPO     share freely (give+give)
  MAMA     nurture deeply, motherly
  TATA     long ago, deep past
  JAJA     keep talking, storytelling
  FIFI     deep earth, grounding
  SISI     label carefully


================================================================================
  9. EXAMPLE SENTENCES
================================================================================

 1. I am happy.
    mi i mola.
    I - BE - joy

 2. The box is here.
    koka i koti.
    box - BE - here

 3. It starts raining.
    wawa a fala.
    rain - BECOME - fall

 4. I go there.
    mi a koto.
    I - MOVE - there

 5. The creature runs (actively).
    bo o hihi.
    creature - DO - run

 6. I want to eat ice-cream.
    mi i moki o di widi.
    I - BE(want) - DO(eat) - ice-treat

 7. I build a bridge with a friend.
    mi o hoka bi koti.
    I - DO(build) - friend - here

 8. I am not sleepy.
    mi na i nini.
    I - NOT - BE - very-sleepy

 9. They became angry and shouted.
    bi a faho, bi o jaho.
    they - BECOME(anger), they - DO(yell)

10. Let's keep talking!
    jojo o jaja h!
    (sing) - DO - talk-talk - (sigh!)


================================================================================
  10. HOW TO BUILD YOUR OWN WORDS & SENTENCES
================================================================================

Step 1: Choose root(s)
  Look up the semantic grid. Pick the roots that capture your meaning.
  HAFA = jump (start+fire), MOMO = love (feel+feel), KOKA = box (place+object)

Step 2: Follow the word-order rule
  Naming a thing? Head-first (category root leads).
  Describing a quality? Modifier-first.

Step 3: Pick the aspect
  i  if it simply is
  a  if it's about to happen or become
  o  if someone is making it happen

Step 4: Add extras
  Reduplication for intensity.
  Paragrammar consonant for emotional tone.

Step 5: Assemble
  (Subject) (na?) (Aspect) (Predicate) (location/time) (paragrammar)
